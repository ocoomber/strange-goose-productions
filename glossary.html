Formats & ratios

16:9
Widescreen. What a TV looks like.
The standard horizontal format — wider than it is tall. Used for YouTube, cinema screens, and anything designed to be watched on a laptop or TV. Not ideal for phones held upright.
Formats & ratios
9:16
Vertical. What your phone looks like when you hold it normally.
The format made for phones held upright. Used for Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts, and Stories. If you're making something primarily for social, this is likely your main format.
Formats & ratios
1:1
Square. Equal width and height.
Historically the Instagram format. Still used for feed posts. Takes up more screen space than widescreen when scrolling, which can help grab attention. Not quite as dominant now that vertical has taken over.
Formats & ratios
4:5
Slightly taller than square. The format that takes up the most space on an Instagram feed without being fully vertical.
A useful middle ground for Instagram feed posts — taller than 1:1, so it takes up more of the screen while scrolling, but not quite as extreme as full 9:16 vertical.
Formats & ratios
4K / 1080p
How sharp the image is. 4K is sharper than 1080p. Both look fine on social media.
These are resolution standards. 1080p (Full HD) is the broadcast standard and looks crisp on any screen. 4K is four times the resolution — relevant if the footage will ever be used on a large screen or cinema. For Instagram or YouTube, the difference is rarely noticeable to viewers.

Platforms

Instagram Reel
Instagram's main short video format. Vertical, up to 90 seconds, shown to people who don't already follow you.
The primary way new people discover you on Instagram right now. The algorithm actively pushes Reels to non-followers, which makes it the most useful format for reach. Shot in 9:16.
Platforms
Instagram Story
A vertical clip that disappears after 24 hours, only shown to people who already follow you.
Stories appear at the top of the app for your existing followers. They're good for updates and reminders to people who already know you — not the format to use if you're trying to reach new people.
Platforms
Instagram feed post
A video (or image) that sits permanently on your Instagram profile grid.
Unlike Stories, feed posts don't disappear. They live on your profile and can be found later. Less dominant for discovery than Reels, but still the thing people see when they land on your page.
Platforms
TikTok
A vertical short-video platform with a very powerful algorithm for reaching new audiences.
TikTok's For You Page shows content to non-followers aggressively — a single video can reach millions of people who've never heard of you. Always vertical (9:16). A video made for Instagram Reels will usually work here too.
Platforms
YouTube pre-roll
The advert that plays before a YouTube video. Viewers can skip it after 5 seconds.
Because most people skip at 5 seconds, the first few seconds are critical. Pre-roll ads are widescreen (16:9) and typically 15–30 seconds. You only pay (in advertising terms) if someone watches past the skip point.
Platforms
YouTube bumper
A 6-second YouTube ad that cannot be skipped.
Short and unavoidable. Because viewers can't skip, every second counts. Bumpers work well for brand recognition — a quick visual impression rather than a full story. Usually used alongside longer ads rather than on their own.
Platforms
YouTube Shorts
YouTube's vertical short-video format, equivalent to TikTok and Reels.
Vertical (9:16), under 60 seconds. If you're making a Reel, you usually have a Shorts version for free — same file, same cut. YouTube's algorithm is also pushing Shorts aggressively to new viewers.
Platforms
In-feed ad
A paid video that appears in someone's social media scroll, looking like normal content.
As opposed to a pre-roll (which plays before something else), in-feed ads appear in the main feed as people scroll. They blend in more than banner ads, which generally makes them more effective.
Platforms

Lengths & versions

Hero film / master
The full-length version of the film. Everything else is edited down from this.
The master is the complete cut — usually 60–90 seconds for a commercial. All shorter versions (30s, 15s, 10s) are made by working from this original. You only need to shoot once to produce multiple lengths.
Lengths & versions
Cut-down
A shorter version of the main film, edited down for a specific use.
A 60-second hero film might have a 30-second cut-down for pre-roll and a 10-second cut-down for a bumper ad. Each one is edited as its own piece — not just a trim — to make sure it works at that length. You get all of these from a single shoot day.
Lengths & versions
Deliverables
The files you receive at the end. All of them.
When a production company talks about deliverables, they mean the complete package of finished files — every format, every length, ready to use. A good deliverables package is named clearly so you know exactly what to upload where.
Lengths & versions
Master file
The highest quality version of the finished film. Keep this safe.
The master file is the full-resolution version of the final edit. You don't upload this anywhere — you upload the platform-optimised versions. But you keep the master in case you ever need to reformat for something new in future.

Production process

Brief
What you tell the production company about what you want.
A brief can be a formal document, an email, or just a conversation. It covers what the video is for, who it's aimed at, what you want people to feel or do after watching it, and any practical requirements. The clearer the brief, the better the result — but you don't need to know all the answers upfront.
Production process
Treatment
The director's written response to your brief — explains their creative idea before anything is made.
A treatment describes the tone, look, structure, and feel of what the director wants to make — before a camera is switched on. Reviewing and approving the treatment is how you make sure you and the director are making the same film.
Production process
Storyboard
A drawn or illustrated plan of what each shot in the film will look like.
Think of it like a comic strip version of the finished film. Each panel represents a shot, showing the framing, the action, and the sequence. Storyboards let you see the shape of the film before the shoot — useful for approving the idea and for the crew to work from on the day.
Production process
Shoot day
The day (or days) when the filming actually happens.
Everything before this is pre-production (planning). Everything after is post-production (editing). The shoot day is when the camera rolls. For most commercial projects, this is one day — which is why preparation beforehand matters.
Production process
Rough cut
The first edit. Not finished. Shown for feedback before the final version.
A rough cut has all the pieces in the right order but won't have final colour, music, or sound polish. It's shown to the client to check the story, pacing, and structure before the finishing work begins. This is the right moment for major feedback — not after picture lock.
Production process
Picture lock
The point at which the edit is final. No more changes to the cut.
Once picture is locked, the colourist and sound designer do their work on a fixed version. Asking for changes after picture lock means redoing that work — which costs time and usually money. This is why the rough cut stage matters.
Production process
Colour grade
Adjusting how the footage looks in terms of colour, contrast, and tone. The difference between "filmed" and "finished."
Raw footage from a camera is intentionally flat so the colourist has maximum control. Grading is where the visual look of the film is created — warm or cold, bright or dark, filmic or clean. It's a significant creative step with a huge effect on the feel of the finished film.
Production process
Voiceover (VO)
A voice heard over the film that isn't visible on screen.
Voiceover narration is recorded separately from the shoot and laid over the edit. It can be used to tell a story, explain something, or add a call to action. A professional VO artist can record remotely — no studio required.
Production process
Supers
Text that appears on screen over the video image.
From "superimposed text." This covers anything from a product name appearing on screen to the small legal disclaimer text that runs at the bottom of some adverts. Also includes lower thirds — the name/title captions you see in interview-style videos.
Production process
GFX / motion graphics
Animated graphics added to the film in post-production. Logos that fly in, animated titles, that kind of thing.
GFX covers anything created in a computer and added over the footage — animated logo reveals, kinetic text, transitions, or visual effects. These are created after the shoot, in post-production, and are often where a brand's visual identity gets applied to the film.

Marketing jargon

Call to action (CTA)
The thing you want someone to do after watching. "Visit our website." "Book now." "Follow us."
Every piece of commercial video should have a clear answer to "and then what?" — that's the call to action. It might be a line of dialogue, a super on screen, a link in the caption, or all three. Without one, you're hoping people know what to do next.
Marketing
Organic
Content posted without paying to promote it. It reaches whoever sees it naturally.
Organic reach depends on your existing following and whether the algorithm picks it up. Good organic content can spread widely — but there's no guarantee. The opposite of organic is paid or boosted, where you pay to put the content in front of a specific audience.
Marketing
Paid / boosted
Content you've paid to show to a specific audience, beyond your existing followers.
Boosting a post means paying the platform to show it to more people — you choose who, based on age, location, interests, and so on. Paid advertising gives you control over reach that organic content can't guarantee.
Marketing
Reach
How many individual people have seen your content.
Reach counts unique viewers — if the same person watches your video five times, that's still a reach of one. It's the measure of how wide your content has spread, as opposed to impressions, which count every individual view including repeats.
Marketing
Impressions
How many times your content has been seen in total, including repeat views.
If 100 people each watch your video twice, that's 100 reach and 200 impressions. Impressions are always equal to or higher than reach. High impressions relative to reach means people are watching more than once, which is usually a good sign.
Marketing
Engagement
Any active response to your content — likes, comments, shares, saves.
Engagement measures how many people interacted with your content, not just watched it. High engagement signals to the algorithm that the content is worth showing to more people. Saves and shares are generally weighted more heavily than likes by most platforms.
Marketing
Brand awareness
How many people know your brand exists.
Awareness-led content is designed to introduce you to new people — it's not trying to make a sale right now, it's trying to make sure that when someone needs what you offer, they've already heard of you. Most short-form commercial video is awareness work.
Marketing
Conversion
When someone does the thing you wanted them to do — buys, books, signs up, clicks.
Conversion is the end goal. A "conversion rate" is the percentage of people who saw your content and then did the thing. High-converting content typically needs a very clear call to action and is often aimed at people who already know who you are.
Marketing
The funnel
The journey from someone not knowing you exist, to them buying from you.
The top of the funnel is awareness (lots of people). The middle is consideration (they're interested). The bottom is conversion (they buy). Most brands focus on the bottom and forget the top — but if no one knows you exist, there's no one to convert.
Marketing
Above the fold
The part of a webpage visible without scrolling. What someone sees the moment they land.
Borrowed from print — broadsheet newspapers put the most important story above the physical fold. Online, it means the first screenful. If your video is embedded on a website, where it sits relative to the fold affects how many people will actually see it.
